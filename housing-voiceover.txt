In 2005, everyone in America knew one thing for certain. House prices only moved in one direction. Banks believed it, regulators believed it, your neighbors believed it. One fund manager in San Jose disagreed. He had a glass eye, a closet full of the same T-shirt, and a habit of reading documents that nobody else on Wall Street opened. He bet more than a billion dollars that the housing market would collapse. For two years, that bet nearly destroyed him. Then 2007 arrived, and his fund made 725 million dollars. His name is Michael Burry.

Burry grew up in San Jose, California, with one working eye. A childhood cancer called retinoblastoma took the left one at age two. The glass replacement made him stand slightly apart from people his whole life. He found numbers easier company than crowds. He went through medical school at Vanderbilt and a residency at Stanford. At night, between hospital shifts, he wrote about stocks on early internet forums. His picks were specific, documented, and consistently ahead of the market. Professional investors started reading a sleep-deprived medical resident for ideas. In 2000, he quit medicine entirely.

He opened a fund and called it Scion Capital. The investor Joel Greenblatt paid him a million dollars for a piece of it. In 2001, the S&P 500 fell almost 12 percent. Scion rose 55. In 2002, the index dropped another 22 percent. Scion gained 16. The next year, he returned 50 percent. Money poured in until he managed more than 600 million dollars. Then, in 2003, he started reading about the American mortgage machine. What he found in those pages kept him up at night.

A mortgage bond bundles thousands of home loans into one giant package. Each bond comes with a prospectus, hundreds of pages of dense legal text. Almost nobody on Wall Street read them. Burry read them by the dozen. Inside, the loans were getting stranger every year. Mortgages issued with no proof of income and no down payment. Teaser rates that started low and doubled after two years. Borrowers qualified for the low rate, never for the real one. The system worked only while prices climbed and refinancing stayed easy. Burry pulled the data on the 2005 loans and ran the resets forward. The cheap years expired in 2007, almost all at once.

He wanted to bet against those bonds, and no instrument existed for it. So he asked the banks to build one. A credit default swap works like an insurance policy on a bond. He would pay a small premium every year the bonds stayed healthy. If the bonds failed, the banks owed him the full insured amount. On May 19, 2005, Deutsche Bank sold him 60 million dollars of swaps. Goldman Sachs followed a week later, happy to take his premiums. The banks treated the money as free. Houses, after all, only went up. By autumn he held positions against roughly a billion dollars of subprime bonds.

Then came the hardest part, waiting. The premiums drained millions from the fund every single year. His investors had signed up for stock picking, and they wanted out. Greenblatt, the man who discovered him, flew in demanding the trade be abandoned. Burry locked part of the money so nobody could withdraw it. Clients threatened lawsuits and called him insane. Through 2006, house prices flattened, and defaults began to tick upward. The banks still marked his swaps as if they were worthless. He wrote long letters to investors explaining the math. Few replied politely. Around this time, his young son was tested for Asperger's syndrome. Reading the diagnostic checklist, Burry recognized every line in himself. The obsession that isolated him was the same one running the trade.

In early 2007, the 2005 mortgages hit their reset dates. Monthly payments doubled across the country. Borrowers who had never proven an income began missing payments in waves. In February, the index tracking subprime bonds started to slide. In June, two Bear Stearns hedge funds stuffed with these bonds collapsed. Suddenly the banks wanted to buy Burry's insurance from him. The same swaps they had laughed at. He sold into the panic, position by position, at enormous prices. Scion finished 2007 up 166 percent. The fund cleared roughly 725 million dollars in profit. Burry personally made about 100 million. From 2000 to 2008, Scion returned 489 percent. The S&P managed about 3.

The other side of the trade was the country. Through 2008, the bonds he had bet against dragged down their owners. Bear Stearns fell in March. Lehman Brothers followed on September 15. Roughly six million American homes went into foreclosure over the next years. Nearly nine million people lost their jobs. Burry watched it from his office, vindicated and miserable at once. His investors collected their winnings. Almost none of them said thank you. In 2008, he returned the money and closed Scion Capital.

Here is the detail worth holding onto. Every document Burry read was public. Any analyst at any bank could order the same prospectuses. The data on missed payments sat in commercial databases, available by subscription. The trade required no inside information, only the patience to read. Wall Street paid thousands of people to watch this exact market. They held the same pages and saw nothing, because the fees were excellent. A man avoiding eye contact in a San Jose office out-read them all.

In 2010, Michael Lewis told the story in a book, The Big Short. In the 2015 film, Christian Bale played Burry, barefoot, drumming to heavy metal. The real Burry kept investing and kept warning about whatever came next. In 2019 he bought into a dying retailer called GameStop. A year later, that stock set off one of the strangest rallies ever recorded. Being early, it turns out, was his only setting. During the housing trade, being early looked exactly like being wrong. For two full years, every quarterly statement said the market had beaten him. The market simply took two more years to confirm what the documents said. So the next time everyone around you knows something for certain, remember that certainty in 2005 came with a price tag of six million homes. And somewhere, someone unfashionable is probably reading the footnotes.
