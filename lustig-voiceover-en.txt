In 1925, one man decided to sell the Eiffel Tower. For scrap metal. To a businessman who believed every word. Then he came back. And sold it again. His name was Victor Lustig. And this is how he did it.

Lustig was born in 1890 in Bohemia — now the Czech Republic. He spoke five languages. He dressed like a banker. And he had never worked an honest day in his life. By 1925 he had run scams across all of Europe. Fake card games. Fake investments. Fake auctions. Small scores. Nothing that would make history. Until he read a newspaper.

May 1925. Paris newspapers ran the same story. The Eiffel Tower needs massive repairs. The government doesn't know what to do with it. Some articles even suggested the tower might be torn down. Lustig read this and had a thought. What if he sold it?

He checked into the Hotel de Crillon — one of the finest hotels in Paris — using fake government papers. Then he had official stationery printed with a fake letterhead: Ministry of Posts and Telegraphs of France. That ministry actually managed the Eiffel Tower at the time. He wrote letters to five of the biggest scrap metal dealers in Paris. The letters were official. Government seal. Formal language. They said: the French government is selling the Eiffel Tower for scrap. The sale is confidential. You are invited to a private meeting.

All five businessmen showed up. Lustig met them as a senior government official. Quiet voice. Expensive suit. Total authority. He explained the situation. The tower is too expensive to maintain. The city needs the money. Sealed bids. Government contract. Maximum discretion required. He then took the businessmen on a tour of the tower. Official car. Official driver. The Eiffel Tower was right there. Seven thousand three hundred tons of iron. Real and enormous.

One of the five men stood out. His name was André Poisson. Poisson had recently moved to Paris. He wanted to become someone in the business world. A tower made of seven thousand tons of iron felt like the opportunity of a lifetime. Lustig noticed Poisson's hunger. He asked him to stay behind after the group meeting. In private, Lustig leaned in and spoke quietly. He confessed: his government salary was very small. A man in his position needed help. He was asking for a bribe. And here is the genius of it. The bribe made the whole thing feel real. Corrupt officials ask for bribes. Fake ones don't need to. Poisson relaxed. Of course. This is how Paris works.

He agreed to the deal. He paid — roughly seventy thousand dollars in today's money — for the Eiffel Tower. Plus the bribe on top. Lustig took the money, packed his bags, and took the train to Vienna.

Poisson arrived the next day to collect his tower. The ministry that sold it to him did not exist. The official named Lustig had never worked for the government. Poisson had bought nothing. He was so humiliated he never went to the police. He couldn't face people knowing he had tried to buy the Eiffel Tower from a stranger in a hotel lobby. So Lustig read about it in exactly zero newspapers.

He waited one month in Vienna. Then he came back to Paris. And did the exact same thing again. New five businessmen. Same hotel. Same fake papers. The second buyer also paid. But this one went to the police. Lustig was already on a train. He escaped to the United States.

In America, he invented a new product. He called it the Money Box. A wooden device that supposedly copied real banknotes. He would load it with two real hundred-dollar bills. The machine would slowly produce a third. The third bill was real too — he had hidden it inside beforehand. He sold these boxes to wealthy people for tens of thousands of dollars each. By the time the buyer discovered it only held two bills, Lustig was in another city.

In 1930, he sold one to Al Capone. Capone gave him fifty thousand dollars to invest. Lustig planned to keep it all. Then he reconsidered. Stealing from Al Capone had a short career path. Two months later he returned every dollar. Said the investment had failed. Capone was so shocked by the honesty he gave Lustig a thousand dollars as a consolation. Lustig had accidentally made money by giving money back.

In 1935, the FBI caught him. Forty-seven charges. Including counterfeiting one hundred and thirty-four million dollars in fake US currency. He escaped from a Manhattan jail. They caught him ten days later. He was sent to Alcatraz. He died there in 1947. On his arrest form, under profession, he wrote: salesman. It was the only true thing he ever told the authorities.

Here is what is worth remembering. Every one of his victims was trying to get something they should not have had. Poisson wanted a private government deal that beat all competitors. The buyers of the Money Box wanted to print unlimited cash. Lustig never forced anyone. He just gave people what they were already looking for. The best con is not the one that tricks the smartest people. It is the one that tricks people who think they are too smart to be tricked. Victor Lustig sold the Eiffel Tower twice and walked away both times. Not because he was the most brilliant man in the room. But because his buyers were too greedy to ask the obvious question. Why would the French government sell the Eiffel Tower in secret?
